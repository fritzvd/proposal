Context

With the rise of digitization and the data it produces, the question of ownership of data becomes a contested issue for agents such as Indigenous people, consumers and countries (Hummel et al. 2021). If the ownership is not clear and personal data cannot always be obtained (Herrmann and Lindemann 2016) it should be clear we should not only look to data governance but also to privacy literacy (Koltay 2016). However, much of privacy literacy literature has used ethnocentric Western-based ideas of governance and regulation that do not take into account the multi-varied ways privacy can be perceived in the Global South (Arora 2019a, 2019b). Digital privacy today faces a lot of threats. Some Big Tech parties have decided to bring a limited version of the internet to the Global South in the last decade “Introducing Project Loon: Balloon-powered Internet Access” (2013).

Problem

Understanding what happens with your data is difficult and ambigiuous in any context (Bartsch and Dienlin 2016), intuitively this would be even more so where internet is free and provided by commercial companies who have an interest in obtaining your personal data. Companies like Meta profit from tracking users online (Wernerfelt et al. 2025). Where the quality or the inner workings of zero-rated services are being assessed Sen et al. (2017) results are mixed. On the other hand zero-rated services have significantly contributed to access to information and appointments for Maternal Health or free messaging for HIV/AIDS medical appointments (Layton and Elaluf-Calderwood 2016). Other critiques of “zero-rating” are issues of net-neutrality and gatekeeping (Galpaya 2017).

Research questions

Main question

How can people in Sub Saharan Africa benefit from being “connected” without sacrificing their privacy?

(Possible this can be more specified towards a Case Study in rural South Africa, or Botswana?)

Sub-questions

  • What initiatives stand out in amlerioating the digital divide?
  • In what way are people aware of the predicament of sharing personal information?
  • What is the benefit of participating in the digital “online world”?
  • What legislation is in place to ensure digital privacy at a national and regional level?
  • What conflicts between legislation and ethics arise concerning privacy?
  • What are main differences when dealing with privacy in Europe and SSA?

The model 6 Private I’s is proposed by Hartman-Caverly and Chisholm (2023) as a way to perceive teach privacy and to recognize that privacy firstly concerns people and not just technology.

According to Rotman (2009) for users to consciously decide what happens with their information they should go from understanding, recognizing, realizing, evaluating to deciding. But this sounds very generic about anything you can teach..

Significance

A study by Prinsloo and Kaliisa (2022) has been held to asses specific sub-topics such as student data privacy researching regulatory frameworks on the African content. The angle here to assess Higher Education Institutes (HEI’s) is helpful as it is a very concrete example and produces very clear results. However HEI’s are also quite an exclusive segment of society that cannot be used to draw broader conclusions. Another study uses a survey in an impressive array of countries but has quite a low sample size, primarily in the generation we call millenials, which might be the most digital and privacy literate(“Personal Data and Privacy Barriers to E-Government Adoption, Implementation and Development in Sub-Saharan Africa” 2019).

This study should be different from both previous efforts.
