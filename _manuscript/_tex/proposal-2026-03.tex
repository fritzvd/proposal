\subsection{Introduction}\label{introduction}

A shift in world order, a challenge with climate change, and more potent
ways to utilize our data with a resurgence of Artificial Intellegince
(AI) in the last few years have us all on the edge of our seat. We are
no longer sure what is real or what is generated (Miller et al. 2023),
we are promised that AI will solve climate change (Woodcock 2024) while
at the same time we are seeing an ``AI Gold Rush'' where more and more
electrical power use is being needed to sustain the growth of AI usage
(Lin et al. 2024).

The permeation of AI in our daily digital lives is almost inescapable.
The biggest communication platforms and work applications have an AI
function embedded, for example Copilot throughout Microsoft Office
Suite, Meta AI in WhatsApp and Google Search giving Gemini generated
results as top result. Signified with ``AI'' or ✨ it is usually a form
of a Large Language Model (LLM) that interprets requests and gives
human-like answers. With a new direction called \emph{Agentic AI} these
LLMs (LLMs) that make the likes of Claude and ChatGPT possible, are used
to create (semi-)autonomous systems that ``reason'' and feedback into
itself to perform the tasks or solutions that it proposes. This marks a
paradigm shift in the use of the web, where not only humans shape its
future (Yang et al. 2025). Because it is so new rules, regulations or
benchmarks are not yet in place and it is ``hard to distinguish genuine
advances from hype'' (Kapoor et al. 2024). The advances made in AI are
hoped to work towards a goal of Artifical General Intelligence (AGI),
bringing ``human-like intelligence'' (Blili-Hamelin et al. 2025).

Talk of being ``falling behind'' (Eder and Sjøvaag 2025) or a ``skill
gap'' (Van Haeften et al. 2024; Chrisinger 2019) or ``losing a race''
(du Toit 2025) fuels anxiety of missing out (Chen 2025; Yu 2025). If AI
automates or replaces human labor instead of augmenting it, it creates
the risk of concentrating wealth and power, the \emph{Turing Trap}
(Brynjolfsson 2022).

Besides the future of labour there are other real and pressing issues
for us to contend with. For example the profound physical impact these
new technologies are having on our environment (Crawford 2021, 49), the
security issues of models with jailbreak attempts Carlini et al. (2021),
nudging political preferences(Cupać, Schopmans, and Tuncer-Ebetürk
2024), reducing the quality of democracy decision-making and the erosion
of trust in legitimate institutions (Miklian and Hoelscher, n.d.).

Young people around the globe are entering a different digital reality
than their parents, where AI is playing a big part. Much of the
adolescent student population is already using tools like ChatGPT (Dai
2025). How then do we avoid the Turing Trap, and increase agency for
(young) humans instead of infringing upon it?

\subsection{Gap}\label{gap}

Much of the research regarding AI has been focussing on improving models
(Du, Yang, and Welleck 2025; Yao et al. 2025), improved productivity in
writing code (Kumar et al. 2025), effects on policy (Khanal, Zhang, and
Taeihagh 2025; Leslie and Perini 2024), impact on education (Dai 2025;
Han, Peng, and Liu 2025; Udeh 2025) on human interactions (Hou et al.
2025), and how it can improve self-advocacy (Register and Ko 2020).

In the course of history, human development has been argued to increase
where agency and liberty increase (Prados de la Escosura 2022; Sen
1999). Agentic AI and AI goals in general, largely seem to focus on
increasing abilities for models or replacing tedious tasks performed by
humans. While research has been done about future projections of human
agency in decision making (Pew Research Center, Anderson, and Rainie
2023) and in education (Mouta, Pinto-Llorente, and Torrecilla-Sánchez
2025) it does not deal with how to increase human agency for humans now.

AI literacy is one field where demystification and explanation are seen
to increase people's insight in systems (Pinski and Benlian 2024) and
help to reduce anthropomorphism (Druga and Ko 2021) which in itself can
lead to false perceptions of AI abilities (Guest et al. 2025).

\subsection{Hook}\label{hook}

\emph{``Technological development has invested the powers that be with
not only more efficient, better and more deadly instruments of coercion
but with the instruments of persuasion decidedly more efficient than
those hitherto used by the political bosses..''} - (Mathur, n.d.)

If we want to create circumstances for technology where humans can
thrive we need to be able to accept a pluralistic view of technology. As
technology is never just neutral nor is it inherently good or bad
(Morrow 2014; Heyndels 2023). Regulation and policy fueled by ideology
(both benefiting the few or the many) has been the driver for the
direction this advancement would take us (Johnson and Acemoglu 2023,
57).

\subsection{Research Questions}\label{research-questions}

How can adolescents achieve increased human agency and freedom in a
world with agentic AI?

\subsubsection{Sub-questions}\label{sub-questions}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  How are students interacting with agentic AI or GenAI?
\item
  How is AI affecting the execution of day-to-day tasks (e.g.~study,
  work, household chores)?
\item
  What beliefs do adolescents have about AI and its capabilities in
  relation to their own capabilities?
\item
  In what ways are AI Literacies affecting these beliefs?
\item
  How do adolescents discern hype from reality?
\item
  In what ways can understanding of these phenomena enhance their
  freedom of choice?
\end{enumerate}
